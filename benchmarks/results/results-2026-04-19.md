Comparators: **flowgate**, **axum 0.7**, **actix-web 4**, raw **hyper 1.x**.

## Environment

- Host: `Linux 6.6.87.2-microsoft-standard-WSL2 x86_64`

- Cores: `22`
- Workers (multi-thread mode): `22`
- Tooling: oha `1.14.0`, rustc `1.94.1`
- Profile: `release` + `lto = "thin"`, `codegen-units = 1`, `opt-level = 3`
- Each cell: 3s warmup, 15s measurement, 3 reps, **median** reported.

**Caveats:** WSL2 host (no kernel pinning, noisy neighbors). Treat results as **relative comparisons**, not absolute throughput numbers.

## Interpretation

### Headline

Flowgate's hot path is **~10–25% slower than axum, actix-web, and raw hyper** across most high-concurrency cells, with the gap widest in single-thread / `c=256` and multi-thread plaintext. At low concurrency (`c=1`) all four frameworks are within noise of each other (~19–21k RPS). Memory footprint is competitive with axum/hyper and well below actix.

### Per-mode observations

**Single-thread, `GET /plaintext`** — the simplest possible path:

| Framework | RPS @ c=64 | RPS @ c=256 | Δ vs flowgate @ c=256 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| flowgate | 106 612 | 100 517 | — |
| axum | 116 091 | 116 259 | +16% |
| actix | 122 275 | 127 558 | +27% |
| hyper | 130 518 | 134 427 | +34% |

Flowgate is the slowest of the four on this route. Raw hyper is the upper bound; Flowgate sits ~25% below it. At this workload every allocation per request shows up.

**Single-thread, `POST /echo`** — body-read + JSON in/out, closer to a real endpoint:

| Framework | RPS @ c=64 | RPS @ c=256 |
|---|---:|---:|
| flowgate | 65 908 | 64 771 |
| axum | 66 739 | 70 925 |
| actix | 79 480 | 106 251 |
| hyper | 78 337 | 99 854 |

Flowgate **matches axum at c=64** but lags under higher concurrency. Actix and raw hyper pull ahead because their body-read paths have less plumbing than Flowgate's `Limited`-wrapped + `body_read_timeout` guarded extractor.

**Multi-thread** — gap widens at scale:

| Framework | Plaintext RPS @ c=256 | Echo RPS @ c=256 |
|---|---:|---:|
| flowgate | 316 719 | 356 013 |
| axum | 409 141 | 375 977 |
| actix | 467 993 | 409 339 |
| hyper | 425 930 | 432 077 |

Flowgate scales with cores, but the per-request overhead compounds: at c=256 multi-thread it's ~24–32% behind axum/hyper on plaintext.

### Where Flowgate wins

- **Binary size (with LTO):** flowgate **1410 KiB** vs actix 5392 KiB — **~4× smaller than actix**, essentially tied with axum (1471 KiB) and slightly heavier than the raw-hyper floor (1166 KiB).
- **Peak RSS:** 3–17 MiB under load, comparable to axum/hyper and consistently below actix.
- **Latency at low concurrency (c=1):** within noise of the others (~50µs p50).

### Plausible sources of the gap (not yet profiled)

These are *hypotheses* based on knowing Flowgate's architecture, not measurements:

1. **`RouteParams` allocation on every request** — matchit returns borrowed params; Flowgate copies into `Vec<(String, String)>` and inserts into request extensions *unconditionally*, even for routes with no path params (the common case).
2. **Middleware chain walker** — `Arc<[Arc<dyn Middleware<S>>]>` indirection applied uniformly even for routes with zero middleware.
3. **`Json<T>` always wraps the body in `Limited` + checks `body_read_timeout`** even when the caller uses stock defaults and nothing is adversarial.
4. **`TokioTimer` wired unconditionally** to support optional header-read timeout. Raw hyper skips the timer wheel by default.

Each of these is a plausible 2–8% win if optimized. Combined, they could close most of the gap with axum.

### Recommendation

**For crates.io v0.2.0:** these numbers are honest and publishable. Don't market Flowgate as "fastest" — lead with **ergonomics, small footprint (4× smaller than actix), embedded-first design, single-crate simplicity**. The perf gap is moderate and matches axum's order-of-magnitude.

**For v0.3:** profile the four hypotheses above. The first two are likely the biggest levers.
